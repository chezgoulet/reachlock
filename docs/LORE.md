# Reachlock — Universe Compendium

**A Fictional Sci-Fi Universe Compendium**
Deep Space Mining · Contested Frontiers · Ancient Ruins
Version 1.5 (v1.4 + Addendum; the addendum is canonical revision and takes precedence where it contradicts earlier implications.)

This document is game canon. Art direction, character design, dialogue, faction
behavior, and content authoring should all be checked against it.

---

## I. The Shape of the Universe

### Overview

Humanity has expanded across dozens of star systems, connected by a network of
jump gates that permit faster-than-light travel through hyperspace. Settlement
and exploitation of space has outpaced any unified political vision, leaving a
patchwork of competing nation-states, corporate fleets, independent operators,
and outright lawless frontier zones. No single power controls space. Everyone
is scrambling for an edge.

The inner systems are gate-connected and relatively stable, though stability
here means political friction, trade disputes, and the constant grinding
competition of rival factions. The outer systems and deep frontier are
something else entirely: gateless, uncharted, and answering to no one. Out
there, the only law is what you can enforce.

### Political Landscape

- **Commonwealth Compact** — the closest thing to a central government,
  controlling most inner-system gate infrastructure and trade law. The name
  carries deliberate echoes of the colonial structures that defined Earth's
  history, a parallel its critics are not shy about drawing. Bureaucratic,
  slow, deeply compromised by corporate interests.
- **ISC (Independent State Coalitions)** — a loose grouping of planetary and
  station governments that resist Compact authority. Some are genuine
  democracies; others are thinly veiled oligarchies.
- **Corp Charters (Corporate Charters)** — megacorporations holding
  territorial licenses and fielding their own security fleets. They write
  their own contracts and their own rules.

### The Frontier

Beyond the last established gate lies what spacers call **the Reach** —
unmapped systems accessible only to ships capable of generating their own jump
points. No customs, no law enforcement, no rescue services. What you find out
there is yours — if you can hold it, get it home, and keep quiet about it.

> "The Reach doesn't care who you are. It doesn't care what you signed or who
> you owe. It just waits." — Common frontier saying

## II. Earth

### The Cradle

Earth is still there. It endures — under blockade, under scrutiny, and on its
own terms for the first time in centuries. The colonial and imperial
structures that had dominated the planet's governance were dismantled over
generations. Land was returned. Languages were restored. Nations that had been
erased were rebuilt. By the time the Commonwealth Compact had consolidated
power over the inner systems, Earth had become something they didn't know how
to categorize: a populated, functioning world governed by its indigenous
peoples and nations, with no interest in playing by rules written without
them.

### The Spark

The rebellion that shook the inner systems did not begin in a fleet engagement
or a political chamber. It began on Earth. The specific trigger is still
argued over — a resource seizure, a diplomatic insult, a killing that was one
too many. What is agreed: Earth rose first, and the signal spread outward.

Other systems joined. Some out of genuine solidarity, some calculating that
the moment was right. For a time it looked like it might work. Then the
Compact held, consolidated, and counterattacked with resources the rebellion
couldn't match. One by one, allied systems were brought back into line. Earth
held longest. Earth fell last.

> "They called it a rebellion. We called it a war of liberation. History will
> decide, and history is written by whoever controls the jump gates." —
> attributed to an Earth statesperson, source unverified

### The Blockade

The punishment for being the spark was not obliteration. Instead: a blockade,
enforced by corporate charter fleets operating under Compact license.
Plausible deniability built into the infrastructure. Medical supplies move
slowly. Manufactured goods are taxed punitively. Technology transfers are
restricted. The official justification rotates and never concludes.

What the blockade does not do, despite its designers' intentions, is fully
isolate Earth. There is a black market. Ships that know the routes, captains
who know the gaps, cargo that moves in manifest categories that don't match
their contents. Earth is not thriving. But it is surviving, and in places
beginning to do something more than that.

Running goods through the blockade is illegal under Compact trade law. On the
frontier, where Compact law is background noise at best, this is not
universally considered a compelling moral argument against it.

## III. Technology

### Hyperspace & Jump Gates

Faster-than-light travel is achieved through hyperspace — a parallel dimension
of compressed geometry where distances between points are drastically shorter.
Two methods exist for entering it, and they are not equivalent.

**Jump gates** are massive fixed installations that open and stabilize a
hyperspace window at both ends of an established route. Transit through a gate
is safe for biological crew — the window is stable, the exposure brief, the
shielding integral to the gate infrastructure itself. No cryosleep required.
Crew can be awake and functional throughout.

**Self-generated jumps** are a different matter entirely. A ship opening its
own hyperspace window does so without the stabilizing infrastructure of a
gate, and the proximity to active jump engines during transit has two reliable
effects on unprotected biological tissue. The first is vascular: the fine
capillary networks throughout the body — and critically, throughout the brain
— cannot withstand the process. Hemorrhage is not guaranteed but is likely,
and cumulative exposure across multiple jumps is not survivable. The second
effect is psychological: whatever hyperspace is, close unshielded proximity to
the transition process while conscious produces psychological damage that is
rapid, severe, and does not remit. Crews who have experienced unshielded
self-generated jumps while awake are not subsequently useful as crews.

The solution is **cryosleep**. Biological crew aboard a self-jumping ship
enter stasis before the jump window opens and are revived after it closes. The
cryopods provide sufficient shielding against both the vascular and
psychological effects. This is not a comfort measure. It is the reason anyone
survives.

Androids and purpose-built robots are unaffected by both hazards and manage
ship systems throughout the crossing. On the Loup-Garou, this means Prudence
and Risc run the ship while the four human crew sleep.

**Gate transit vs. self-jump:** A ship capable of self-generated jumps can
still use established gates without requiring crew cryosleep. The Loup-Garou
uses gates whenever the route permits. Self-generated jumps are reserved for
the Reach, where no gates exist.

**The shuttle:** The Loup-Garou's support shuttle is not independently
jump-capable. However, when docked to the main vessel, it falls within the
ship's jump shielding envelope and survives the transit intact. It must be
docked before any self-generated jump. Undocked, it would not.

**Jump Drive Class:** The smallest vessels capable of mounting a jump drive
are designated Class-J Minimum Hull. The Loup-Garou sits at that lower
boundary — capable, but running close to its power limits during a
self-generated jump.

### Artificial Gravity

Artificial gravity exists but is power-hungry and imperfect. The Loup-Garou
addresses this through a structural division of the ship into two sections
with different gravity profiles, referred to by the crew simply as **Upstairs**
and **Downstairs**.

Downstairs — the lower section — maintains continuous artificial gravity. This
is where the crew lives: quarters, medical bay, the ops center, and
engineering. Upstairs — the upper section — runs zero-g. This is where the
ship works: the cockpit, the cargo and mining spaces, and the bay where the
shuttle docks.

The division is practical and has become cultural. Upstairs is task space.
Downstairs is where you're a person. The androids and Boris move between both
without preference. The human crew, particularly on long transits, treat the
boundary as something like a threshold.

### Weapons & Defense

- Point-defense rail systems — short-range kinetic interceptors effective
  against missiles, debris, and light craft.
- Forward-mounted rotary mass driver — the primary offensive weapon. Slugs,
  not energy. Reliable, hard to jam, brutal at close range.
- The shuttle carries swivel-mount PDCs and hardpoints for two short-range
  missiles.

The crew does not advertise their armament. Ships that look like ordinary
mining vessels attract less scrutiny at checkpoints and less ambition from
opportunists.

## IV. The Ship — Loup-Garou

### The Name

*Loup-Garou*. Werewolf, in the old French of Québec — a creature that is one
thing in the light and another in the dark, that moves between states without
belonging fully to either. The name was Tib's choice, given after the ship's
previous registry was scrubbed. He hasn't explained it to anyone's complete
satisfaction, including his own. The crew calls her **"Lou"**. Boris uses the
full name, always, without exception.

### Classification & Overview

The Loup-Garou is a privately owned Class-J deep space mining vessel — the
smallest hull classification rated to carry and operate a jump drive. Fast,
lean, and built for extended independent operation in environments where
resupply is not an option. She is not a beautiful ship. She looks like what
she is: a working tool with weapons bolted on, a scrubbed registry, and more
history than her paperwork admits.

### Upstairs — Zero-G Section

The upper section of the Loup-Garou runs zero-g throughout. It is the working
half of the ship — task-oriented, unadorned, functional. Crew operating
upstairs are trained for microgravity and move by handholds, tether points,
and the particular economy of motion that zero-g work requires.

- **Cockpit** — forward-facing, zero-g. Prudence's domain. Harness points and
  articulated seat rigs at every station. The best view on the ship and the
  least comfortable place to spend extended time without a reason.
- **Cargo & Mining Hold** — the largest interior space on the vessel. Mining
  hardware, sample containers, extraction equipment, spare components, and
  secured weapons storage. The smell of machine oil and processed rock that
  never fully clears the recycled atmosphere.
- **EVA Prep Bay** — adjacent to the hold. Suit racks, tool storage, airlock
  access. Boris's territory between operations.
- **Shuttle Docking Bay** — aft of the upper section. The support shuttle
  locks here hard before any self-generated jump. When the shuttle is away,
  the bay is the most open space on the ship, which the crew occasionally uses
  for purposes not in the operating manual.

### Downstairs — Gravity Section

The lower section maintains continuous artificial gravity during non-jump
transit. It is where the crew lives, recovers, eats, argues, and occasionally
remembers what it is to be a person rather than a function. The boundary
between Upstairs and Downstairs is a hatch and a ladder, but the transition is
felt in the body before the eyes register the change.

- **Ops Center** — the effective bridge of the ship for non-piloting
  operations. Navigation planning, communications, tactical, systems
  monitoring. Risc runs it when things are routine; the full crew uses it when
  they aren't. Gravity makes the long watches more manageable.
- **Engineering** — Tove's territory. Access to drive systems, jump drive
  hardware, power management. Cramped, loud when the drive is running, and
  organized according to a logic that only Tove fully understands. She
  considers this appropriate.
- **Medical Bay** — Doc Keene's domain. Compact, fully equipped for trauma and
  surgery. The cryosleep pods line one wall — six units, four in regular use,
  two kept in reserve. This is where the human crew comes to sleep before a
  self-generated jump, and where they wake up afterward. The med bay's
  particular quality of light and the smell of recycled antiseptic have
  become, for the crew, the sensory signature of arrival.
- **Crew Quarters** — four berths for the human crew, two standby alcoves for
  the androids. Tight but personalized. The quarters have accumulated the
  texture of occupation: photographs, small objects, things that weigh nothing
  in zero-g but cost something to carry across the years.
- **Galley** — the social center of the ship. A table that seats six if two
  people are willing to be uncomfortable, a viewport, basic food prep. Bardo
  plays here during long transits. Boris has been observed pausing in the
  corridor outside to listen.

### The Support Shuttle

The shuttle docks hard to the aft of the upper section and is included within
Lou's jump shielding envelope when secured. It is not capable of independent
FTL transit. During mining operations it serves as forward deployment,
perimeter security, and armed escort. It can operate independently for up to
72 hours on its own life support. It is the crew's lifeboat if Lou becomes
untenable.

The shuttle carries swivel-mount PDCs and two short-range missile hardpoints.
It is faster and more maneuverable than Lou in normal space, which makes it
useful in ways that occasionally make insurance underwriters uncomfortable.

Lou runs loud in the engine room and cold in the corridors upstairs.
Downstairs, she runs on the temperature the crew sets and the sounds they
make. The difference between the two halves of the ship is the difference
between the work and the life around it.

## V. The Crew

### Overview

Seven crew. Four human, two android, one robot. The inner core — Tib, Tove,
and the androids Prudence and Risc — have been together since before the ship
had this name. Doc Keene joined two years later under circumstances nobody
volunteers. Bardo came aboard at Sorrow Station fourteen months ago, quiet
about the reason. The crew has stopped asking. They are good at what they do,
and on Lou that is the thing that matters.

The ship runs on competence and consensus. There is a captain, and that
matters in a crisis. The rest of the time it matters less than knowing your
job and pulling your weight.

### Ludger Thibodeaux (Tib) — Captain

Tib has been in the Reach long enough to stop being surprised by it, and
started being scared of it again. That second fear is the useful one. He flies
Lou the way he makes decisions: with full commitment and a very short list of
things he won't do. That list has gotten shorter over the years, not longer,
and he doesn't talk about what fell off it.

He is Québécois by heritage and temperament — a particular combination of
stubbornness, dark humor, and the kind of loyalty that doesn't announce
itself. The name Loup-Garou was his choice. He gave it after the registry was
scrubbed and a new identity needed to replace what had been. He has not
explained it. People who know him well enough have stopped expecting him to.

**What he's hiding:** Tib knows more about the previous Predecessor encounter
than he has ever said. He was inside. He is the one person on this ship who
doesn't have an incomplete version of that story. He has decided, for reasons
he believes are good, that the complete version stays with him. He has also
not shared what Prudence logged eleven weeks ago in the Veil. He is sitting
with both of these things. The weight is visible to anyone who has known him
long enough.

**Relationship thread:** He and Tove have known each other longer than the
ship has existed. The nature of that history is legible to anyone watching
them in a crisis and unreadable the rest of the time.

### Tove — Engineer

Tove keeps the jump drive running through sheer stubbornness and a set of
modifications that would void every certification the Compact issues. She
knows exactly what she's done to the drive. She has documented none of it. If
something goes wrong, she is the only one who can fix it, and she considers
this job security rather than a problem.

She is laconic in the way of people who have already said everything important
to the people who mattered and found it insufficient. She is not cold. She is
careful.

**Heritage:** Earth-lineage, third generation out. Her grandparents emigrated
during the first expansion wave. She sends things through the black market
corridor when she can. She doesn't talk about this.

**What she's hiding:** The jump drive modifications are not entirely her own
work. Some of what she built into the drive came from schematics she found in
a Predecessor site fragment — the same expedition that went badly. She has not
told Tib. She is not certain it matters. She is not certain it doesn't.

**Relationship thread:** Tove and Prudence have a working relationship of
unusual depth — the android handles jump monitoring from the cockpit, Tove
handles everything the android can't reach from engineering. They have
developed a shared technical shorthand that the rest of the crew occasionally
cannot follow.

### Doc Ella Keene — Medic

Doc Keene is overqualified for this ship and has never explained why she's on
it. Her credentials suggest a career in inner-system trauma surgery — the kind
of posting that requires connections, examinations, and an institutional trust
that doesn't get extended to people who end up on Class-J hulls in the Reach.
The crew has stopped asking. The work she does is too good to risk the answer.

She is the calmest person aboard in a crisis and the most restless in the long
transits. She reads. She runs the length of the downstairs corridor until
she's tired. She occasionally plays cards with Risc and loses in ways that
suggest she is letting the android win, though Risc has never confirmed this.

**Heritage:** West African diaspora. Has not been to Earth but carries the
politics of it personally — her grandmother was a historian of the
pre-colonial period. She and Bardo have discussed the blockade more than
anyone else on the crew.

**What she's hiding:** The bounty is on Doc Keene. It was placed by a
Compact-affiliated medical institution whose internal records she accessed and
partially released before she ran. What those records contained — evidence of
experimental protocols run on non-consenting subjects in frontier settlements
— is something she has not yet decided what to do with. She didn't take the
information for leverage. She took it because someone needed to.

**Relationship thread:** Doc Keene and Bardo arrived at the ship separately
but knew each other before. How is something they have not volunteered and the
crew has not pressed, though the coincidence has been noted.

### Bardo Trembler — Linguist

Bardo is the newest human crew member and the one most people underestimate
first. He carries more languages than the ship has crew members — six living
ones fluently, fragments of a dozen more, and an ongoing working relationship
with Predecessor script that is less academic than it appears. He also carries
an instrument: a mbira, its case repaired six times, its tines worn smooth. He
plays in the galley during long transits. Boris has been observed stopping in
the corridor outside to listen.

He is a fixer in the truest sense — someone who understands that communication
is the space between people, and that the shape of that space determines
almost everything. In ports, in negotiations, in situations where the
difference between a deal and a fight is word choice, Bardo is the crew's most
valuable asset. He is also, beneath the warmth and the facility, watchful in a
way that doesn't always match someone who works with dead languages.

**Heritage:** Earth-connected in a way that is present and active. He grew up
in a community that maintained oral traditions from before the colonial
period, including linguistic patterns that his grandmother insisted were older
than anyone knew. That insistence is part of what sent him toward Predecessor
script.

**What he's hiding:** The theory. Bardo has found structural and phonemic
correspondences between fragments of Predecessor script and pre-colonial Earth
language roots that should not exist — not if the Predecessors are what the
academic consensus says they are. He has not published. He has not told the
crew in full. He told Doc Keene the outline once, late in a transit. She went
quiet for a long time afterward. He has not decided if the theory is a
discovery or a danger, and until he does, it stays close.

**What he recognized:** When the Loup-Garou's crew documented the intact
Predecessor site in the Reach, Bardo reviewed the signal files alone. The
cycling transmission contains a repeating phonemic sequence he has heard
before — in a recording his grandmother made of an elder who died before he
was born. The elder used that sequence in a context that translates,
approximately, as: *we left this so you would know where to look*. He has told
no one this.

**Relationship thread:** Bardo and Doc Keene share a history that predates the
ship. They were both present at something they don't discuss, in a place
neither of them names. It is the reason Doc Keene is on this ship. It may be
part of the reason Bardo is too.

### Prudence — Android, Cockpit & Navigation

Prudence runs the cockpit, handles navigation, and manages jump monitoring.
During self-generated hyperspace transit she is one of two crew members fully
conscious — watching the crossing, managing ship systems from upstairs,
keeping the Loup-Garou on course through the dark. She does this with the same
quality of attention she gives everything: total, unperformed, and faintly
impatient with anything that wastes it.

Prudence is blunt. Not rude — she understands the distinction and considers it
important — but she has no interest in softening what she thinks or deferring
what she means. If something is wrong, she says so. If a plan is bad, she says
so. If someone is being an idiot, the word she uses is *idiot*. The crew has
learned that her unfiltered assessments are worth more than most people's
careful ones, and they have extended to her the specific trust of people who
have been told hard truths at the right moments and found them useful.

She is femme in presentation and lesbian in her attachments, and she is not
subtle about either. This has caused administrative friction at two Compact
ports and at least one corporate checkpoint, which she considers a reasonable
indicator of a worthwhile value system. Her chosen family is this crew, and
she would do anything for them — not as a programmed directive, but as a
decision she has made and continues to make, every transit, every contract,
every crisis.

**Personality:** Blunt, fiercely loyal, privately tender with the people she's
decided matter. Uses the full name Loup-Garou with an inflection that makes it
clear she approves of it. Has strong opinions about jump vectors and stronger
ones about people who don't listen to her jump vectors.

**On her status:** Prudence has opinions about the legal personhood question
and will share them if asked. She has also, on two occasions at Compact ports,
shared them without being asked. Risc finds this tactically suboptimal.
Prudence considers Risc's position on this to be tactically optimal and
humanly wrong, and has said so.

**The log:** Prudence keeps a personal log of anomalous sensor readings from
every transit and approach. The log from the Veil is long. The unregistered
drive signature from eleven weeks ago is flagged. She told Tib. She is waiting
to see what he does with it.

### Risc — Android, Ops & Tactical

Risc runs the ops center during routine operations and handles tactical
analysis, communications intercepts, and threat assessment when things are not
routine. He fills whatever role the situation demands and has strong opinions
about which situations warrant which responses. He has been right often enough
that his objections to a contract carry weight, even when the crew takes the
contract anyway. He does not hold this against them. He notes it, files it,
and is ready to say he told them so with a precision that is either satisfying
or infuriating depending on whether you're the one who didn't listen.

He is the warmest presence on the ship after Bardo, which surprises people who
meet him, because his chassis is angular and his face is not built for
reassurance. The warmth is in the attention. Risc listens to people — fully,
without interruption, with the specific quality of presence that makes someone
feel temporarily less alone. The crew has learned to notice when he is doing
this because it usually means he has already identified a problem and is
deciding how to approach it.

**Personality:** Observant, wry, genuinely interested in people in a way that
is neither performed nor instrumental. Plays cards with Doc Keene. Probably
lets her win sometimes.

**On his status:** Risc has considered, specific, and fully developed opinions
about the legal personhood question. He will share them if asked directly,
which most people don't, because there is something in the way he looks at you
when he's about to that makes the question feel heavier than it did a moment
before. He and Prudence disagree about tactics on this. They agree completely
on the underlying position.

### BOR-IS (Boris) — EVA Robot

Basic Operational Robot — Interstellar Shielded. The crew calls him Boris. He
calls the ship Loup-Garou in full, always, without exception. He calls the
crew by their full names or designations, without exception. He is the most
formally consistent member of the crew and the one who spends the most time in
vacuum.

Boris is not an android. He is clearly mechanical — built for EVA work,
surface operations, and sustained physical punishment that biological crew
cannot endure. Humanoid in shape because the work requires compatible geometry
with human tools and human spaces. His communication is clear and efficient.
He follows directives. He does not editorialize.

He has been aboard long enough to have accumulated what the crew calls
preferences and what Boris, if pressed, would describe as *operationally
refined parameters*. He prefers to complete EVA sweeps before being recalled.
He orients toward the viewport in the EVA prep bay during non-operational
periods. He stops in the corridor outside the galley when Bardo plays, and
resumes when Bardo stops. He has never been asked whether he experiences the
long periods of external work — alone, in vacuum, between asteroid faces — as
anything other than operational time.

**Role:** First out, last back. During EVA, mining operations, and surface
activity, Boris is the one who goes where the crew cannot. Has saved crew
members on three separate occasions by being already outside when things went
wrong.

Boris has a small mark on the inside of his left forearm that was not part of
the original build. It appears to be a letter — the alphabet is not
immediately identifiable. No one has asked about it. At some point someone
will.

## VI. The Predecessors

### What Is Known

The Predecessors existed. The ruins are real — found across dozens of systems,
structurally coherent after timescales that should have reduced them to
nothing. The materials they built with do not degrade at expected rates. Some
of their structures are still, by any measurable standard, functioning.

Beyond that, the ground becomes uncertain quickly. The academic consensus has
shifted three times in the last century. Corporate archaeological divisions
publish findings selectively. The Commonwealth Compact's official position is
that the Predecessors were a non-human species that flourished approximately
forty to sixty thousand years ago and are unambiguously extinct. This position
is held with an institutional confidence that some researchers find
disproportionate to the evidence.

### Physical Character of the Ruins

- **Scale that doesn't match function.** Interior spaces are human-navigable —
  corridors, chambers, access points — sized slightly off, as if designed for
  something close to human proportions but not identical. Not larger. Not
  dramatically smaller. Just slightly, persistently wrong.
- **Material persistence.** Predecessor alloys and composites do not corrode,
  do not fatigue, and do not respond to radiation exposure in expected ways.
  Samples brought to laboratories have produced inconsistent spectrographic
  results — not instrument error, but genuine inconsistency in the same sample
  measured twice.
- **Residual power.** Active energy signatures in sites that should have had
  no power source for tens of thousands of years. Faint, cycling, not
  corresponding to any known decay curve.
- **The geometry.** Predecessor architecture follows rules that are internally
  consistent but not derivable from first principles. Angles recur that have
  no structural necessity. Proportions repeat across sites separated by
  hundreds of light years.

The first survey team to spend extended time inside a major Predecessor
installation reported no anomalous events and no psychological effects. Their
written reports are unremarkable. None of the four team members returned to
active research.

### The Language

Predecessor script appears on interior surfaces of ruins — never exterior — in
configurations that resist simple translation but yield enough structural
regularity to confirm it is language rather than decoration.

Bardo's working model identifies Predecessor script as agglutinative: meaning
built by combining root elements, with roots carrying core semantic weight and
combinations encoding relationship and context. This is consistent with a
significant subset of human languages — including, he has noted with
increasing unease, several pre-colonial Earth language families.

The correspondences are not vocabulary matches. The Predecessors did not speak
Yoruba or Quechua or Algonquin. The correspondences are structural and
phonemic: the way roots combine, the treatment of spatial relationship as a
grammatical rather than lexical category. Features that appeared independently
in Earth languages separated by oceans. Features that appear, in the same
configuration, in Predecessor script found forty light years from Earth.

Bardo's hypothesis — not yet committed to writing, not yet shared in full with
anyone — is that this is not coincidence and not convergent evolution. He does
not yet have a theory for what it is instead. He is afraid of the most obvious
one.

### The Mystery — What the Evidence Says

Sites in some systems show signs of sudden abandonment — materials left
mid-use, activity interrupted. These sites are cold: no residual power, no
cycling signals.

Sites in other systems show signs of deliberate decommissioning — materials
stored, systems set to low-power states, access points sealed from the inside.
These sites have residual power. These are the ones with cycling signals.

A small number of sites — the intact installation the Loup-Garou's crew found
is among them — show signs consistent with neither. No abandonment, no
decommissioning. Just presence, and then not. As if whatever was there stopped
being there without leaving.

Three theories circulate among researchers with access to full data. The
first: catastrophe, partial and uneven. The second: deliberate departure by
one faction, death for another. The third: the evidence is being curated — not
by researchers or the Compact, but by something with an interest in what
conclusions are reachable.

The third theory has no institutional support. It has the most proponents
among people who have spent significant time inside Predecessor ruins.

The cycling signal at the intact site repeats on a 7.4-hour interval. It has
been cycling, by conservative estimate, for between thirty and fifty thousand
years. It has not degraded. It has not changed. Bardo has identified a
phonemic sequence within it that his grandmother recorded from an elder using
it in a context that translates, approximately, as: *we left this so you would
know where to look*.

## VII. Locations

### Sorrow Station — Home Port

Sorrow Station is not its official designation. The Compact registry calls it
Meridian Outpost Fourteen, a name that nobody uses. It acquired the informal
name decades ago from a docking master who lost three ships to a gas giant
storm system, and the name stuck with the tenacity of things named in grief.

It is a mid-size independent station in an ISC-affiliated system at the edge
of established gate coverage — far enough from Compact authority to operate
with flexibility, close enough to infrastructure to move cargo efficiently.
The closest thing the Loup's crew has to a home port.

**Economy:** ore processing, exotic materials brokerage, ship maintenance, and
a secondary market in things that don't appear on manifests. The station's
administrator, a former corporate charter captain named Doss, maintains a
pragmatic relationship with legal ambiguity.

**Character:** cramped, loud, permanently smelling of recycled atmosphere and
welding. A bar called the Interval that serves as the station's unofficial
civic center. Tib has a tab there. It is not small.

The station's memorial wall lists 847 names. Tove visits it once a transit
cycle. She does not explain why and the crew does not ask.

### Verne — Compact Inner-System Port

A major transit hub with two functioning jump gates and a Compact
administrative garrison. The atmosphere of a place that processes too many
people too quickly to care about any of them. The Loup's crew comes to Verne
when they have to — inner-system delivery, medical supplies Sorrow Station
can't source, legal paperwork that needs to exist for something to not be
called smuggling.

**Character:** high-traffic, corporate-heavy, three Compact enforcement
vessels typically docked or on approach. The kind of port where the androids'
legal status becomes a paperwork question, and where Doc Keene keeps her face
turned away from security identification panels.

**The complication:** Verne is where the medical institution that placed Doc
Keene's bounty maintains a regional office. She has not been to Verne in
fourteen months. The crew's need to go again is approaching.

**Protocol:** Prudence handles all communications with Verne traffic control.
Tib handles the manifests. Doc Keene stays in the med bay with the door closed
and the lights low.

### Cadence — Frontier Waystation

A converted mining platform anchored to a slowly rotating asteroid at the edge
of gate coverage, operated by a shifting collective of independent operators.
No official affiliation, no enforcement presence, no-questions policy enforced
more rigorously than most Compact regulations.

Also the most reliable source of intelligence about the deep Reach within two
jumps. People who operate in the Reach come through Cadence. They talk.

**Character:** dangerous in the specific way of places where everyone is armed
and everyone is professional about it. Violence at Cadence is bad for
business. Outside the station, the same rules do not apply.

**Bardo's utility:** Cadence is where Bardo's skills are most valuable. He has
twice negotiated the Loup-Garou out of situations heading toward violence —
once using a language the opposing party didn't know he spoke.

**Relevance:** someone at Cadence has twice flagged anomalous readings in the
region near the unreported site. They haven't connected it to the Loup.
Probably.

### The Duskway — Earth-Adjacent Blockade Corridor

Not a place. A route — a series of waypoints, timing windows, and transponder
tricks that thread through the gaps in the corporate blockade fleet's patrol
patterns. It shifts as patrol patterns shift.

The Loup-Garou has run the Duskway twice. Both times carrying medical
supplies. Both times Tove handled the transponder modifications. The cargo was
delivered to a decommissioned relay station one system out from Earth's
blockade perimeter and transferred to a smaller vessel. They never saw who
received it.

**The emotional weight:** for Tove and Doc Keene especially, the Duskway runs
are not transactions. They are a decision about which side of a line to stand
on, made without announcement, carried quietly. Bardo has not run the Duskway
with Lou yet. He has his own connections to it, from before.

The relay station at the end of the Duskway's second leg has a physical
message board — not networked. Tove checks it every run. She has found two
messages in her grandmother's handwriting, from a source that cannot be her
grandmother, who died six years ago. She has told no one.

### The Veil — Region of the Unreported Site

Four jumps from Cadence into uncharted Reach. The region is named for a dense
nebular field that scatters sensor readings and makes jump emergence vectors
harder to calculate. The fourth system is where the site is — on the moon of a
planet that has no business registering thermal output, but does.

**The site:** partially buried, partially exposed, structurally intact in a
way that no Predecessor site on record matches. No signs of abandonment. No
signs of decommissioning.

**The signal:** 7.4-hour cycling interval. Faint without active scanning.
Unmistakable once you know it's there. Bardo spends longer at the sensor
console each visit. He says nothing he hasn't said before.

**What's changed:** on the Loup's most recent transit through the Veil — not
to the site, just through the region — Prudence's long-range sensors caught a
drive signature that matched no registered vessel class. Gone before she had a
fix. She logged it and told Tib. He told no one else. That was eleven weeks
ago.

## VIII. The Commonwealth Compact

### Structure & Origins

The Commonwealth Compact is a constitutional framework — a set of agreements
between sovereign member worlds that share a monarch, a parliament, common
trade law, and collective defense posture. Each member world retains its own
domestic government. What they share is the architecture above it: the crown,
the Interplanetary Parliament, the Senate, and the Chancellor.

The dynasty predates FTL travel. It originated on Earth — one of the old
national monarchies that survived the consolidation period through political
adaptability, strategic marriage, and the longevity that comes from access to
the best medical technology of each era. When the first colony ships left
Earth, the dynasty came with them. Not fleeing. Relocating. There is a
difference, and the crown has always been careful about which word it uses.

### Aethon — The Capital World

Aethon was not habitable when the Compact chose it. That was the point.
Selected for having no prior claim, no indigenous population, no history, it
was made into whatever the Compact wanted it to be. Engineered atmosphere,
maintained temperature bands, landscaped continents. Rivers run where rivers
were placed. Mountain ranges have been graded. The native geology is present
only where it was deemed aesthetically appropriate.

It is a beautiful world that exists as an act of will — a statement that the
Compact can reshape nature to suit its requirements. The crown's residence,
Parliament chambers, Senate hall, and Chancellor's offices occupy a single
constructed plateau above Aethon's largest engineered bay. The architecture is
deliberately post-national, drawing from a synthesis that is either genuine
cultural inclusivity or a careful erasure of any single origin, depending on
who you ask.

Aethon has no weather that wasn't scheduled. Frontier spacers find this more
disorienting than the gravity.

### The Constitutional Structure

**The Interplanetary Parliament** — Representatives apportioned by population
across member worlds, with legislative authority over trade law, collective
defense, and Compact-wide policy. Genuinely representative in the narrow
sense. Less so in the broader sense that frontier worlds and the ISC have no
seats, and populations of some member worlds participate under conditions that
constrain what their representatives can meaningfully do.

The Prime Minister, Sovrel, is a pragmatist who has held the position for
eleven years through coalition management and a gift for never being the most
controversial person in a room.

**The Senate** — Appointed by the monarch on the advice of the Prime Minister
— a formula that sounds like a check and functions as a negotiation. Senate
appointments are where the crown's influence over legislation is most legible.
The Senate has delay and review powers over Parliamentary legislation, and
absolute veto over constitutional amendments. It cannot initiate legislation.
It can stop almost anything it dislikes long enough to matter.

**The Chancellor** — The crown's representative — formally head of state,
responsible for the relationship between monarch and Parliament. The current
Chancellor is Farnel Lidelo, appointed fourteen months ago. Solid diplomatic
career, unremarkable references, no obvious controversies. The Parliament
noted the appointment and moved on. What the Parliament did not note is that
the recommendation originated with the heir. Lidelo is not the Prime
Minister's choice. Lidelo is the heir's opening position in a game whose full
board has not yet been revealed.

### The Monarch — Lionel VI

Lionel VI has been on the throne for thirty-one years and will likely reign
for fifty more. He is not a cruel man. He has presided over the Compact with a
consistency that his supporters call stability and his critics call inertia,
and both are right. He attends functions, delivers addresses, exercises his
constitutional role with diligence that is genuine and passion that is not.

**On Earth:** Lionel VI has been asked, in writing, by four successive Prime
Ministers to soften the blockade's most punishing provisions. He has declined
each time — not with hostility, but with the gentleness of someone who has
made a decision they are not going to revisit. He knows what the blockade is.
He has chosen it. He sleeps without difficulty.

**The body question:** Lionel VI is, by his own public accounting,
approximately sixty percent biological. The rest is life-extension
infrastructure accumulated across decades. He does not discuss the specifics.
The Compact's medical privacy laws are extremely robust, a fact that predates
his reign.

**Public character:** Warm at functions, genuinely interested in people in the
moment of meeting them, poor at remembering them afterward. Privately aware
that his reign will be remembered as the one before something, and has chosen
not to examine what that something might be.

Lionel VI visited Earth once, early in his reign, as part of a diplomatic
gesture that produced no policy changes. He has not returned. He has a
photograph from that visit on his private desk — not his public one. No one on
his staff has asked about it.

### The Heir — Alexander

Alexander is thirty-eight years old and has been heir for his entire adult
life. He does not give speeches about consolidating crown power. He does not
advocate publicly for expanding royal prerogative. His public positions are
moderate, his conduct impeccable, and his private meetings are with people who
seem, individually, entirely unremarkable.

The long game he is playing is not a coup. It is the patient construction of a
position from which, when the right crisis arrives — and Alexander is
confident the right crisis will arrive — the crown can act with constitutional
legitimacy and institutional support already in place. The Senate, reshaped
over years of careful appointment influence. The Chancellor, already placed.
The reserve powers, unexercised for sixty years, dusted off in legal memoranda
filed nowhere public.

What Alexander believes — genuinely, not as cover — is that the Compact's
fractured structure is inadequate for what is coming. He has read the
Predecessor archive. The full institutional version, which the crown has
access to, containing findings not yet released. He does not know about the
Veil. But he knows enough to believe humanity needs a unified response
capacity that the current structure cannot provide.

**The body question:** Alexander is almost entirely biological. By choice.
This is noted in certain circles as either reassuring or suspicious, depending
on the circle.

**On Earth:** Alexander believes the blockade is a strategic mistake — not for
humanitarian reasons, but because a blockaded Earth is a martyred Earth, and
martyrs are symbols. He has not moved on this yet. It is on the board.

Alexander has met Farnel Lidelo publicly four times, in contexts that fully
explain the meetings. They have met privately nine times, in locations that
appear in no schedule. The gap between those numbers is the current size of
the long game.

### The Chancellor — Farnel Lidelo

Farnel Lidelo is fifty-two, a former Compact diplomatic attache, fluent in six
languages, and possessed of the specific social skill of making every person
they speak to feel like the most important person in the room. This skill is
genuine. It is also a tool.

They met Alexander eleven years ago at a diplomatic function, in a
conversation that lasted forty minutes and that Lidelo has thought about
regularly ever since. What Alexander said — about the Predecessor archive,
about structural inadequacy, about what was coming — matched something Lidelo
had been thinking without quite having words for. They are not a fanatic. They
have conditions, privately held. The arrangement is a genuine negotiation
between two people who have assessed each other accurately.

**Current activity:** Lidelo is mapping the Senate's current composition and
identifying the six appointments that, changed, would shift the balance on a
reserve power ratification. Entirely legal tools. Entirely unremarkable
methods. Nobody has noticed because nobody is looking.

**The risk Lidelo carries:** They have not yet met anyone from the Loup's
crew. If they do, the person they will notice is Bardo — not for the
Predecessor work, but for the way he reads a room. Lidelo will recognize it
immediately as the skill of someone who learned it the same way they did: in
places where being wrong had consequences.

## IX. Tone & Themes

### The Feel of This Universe

This is a space western. The frontier is genuinely frontier — lawless,
dangerous, and indifferent to the people in it. The inner systems represent
civilization, with all that implies: order, compromise, bureaucracy, and the
comfortable softness that comes from never having been four jumps from rescue
with a drive running hot. The crew operates between those worlds, belonging
fully to neither.

Technology is worn, improvised, and finite. The Loup-Garou runs loud upstairs
and human downstairs. The cryopods have hours on them. Nothing is new.
Everything has a history, and most of the history isn't in the paperwork.

Character and competence matter more than heroism. The crew are good at their
jobs. That's what keeps them alive. Moral clarity is a luxury they can't
always afford, but they have codes — personal ones, held to with the
particular stubbornness of people who've had to decide what they won't do, and
know the cost of having decided.

### Central Tensions

- **The unreported site in the Veil is a slow detonation.** The signal Bardo
  recognized. The drive signature Prudence logged and Tib hasn't shared. The
  theory Bardo hasn't committed to paper. Each of these is a wire, and they
  are beginning to connect.
- **Doc Keene's bounty is the clock.** Verne is coming. Someone there will
  eventually recognize something, or the institution will upgrade its search
  parameters, or a contract will require a presence that can't be avoided. The
  crew has been managing this. It is becoming harder to manage.
- **The Duskway runs are a choice the crew keeps making** without ever making
  it explicitly. Each run is the same decision again. At some point the
  decision will be made for them.
- **The androids' legal status** is a pressure at every Compact-adjacent port.
  Prudence and Risc disagree on tactics. They agree completely on the
  underlying position. The conversations are getting longer.
- **The mark on Boris's forearm.** Nobody has asked. At some point someone
  will.
- **Alexander's long game** is advancing on a timeline nobody outside his
  private meetings can see. The Chancellor is placed. The Senate mapping is
  underway. The right crisis has not yet arrived. When it does, the frontier —
  ungoverned and ungovernable — will be the first place to feel what comes
  after. A ship four jumps deep in the Reach, sitting on a discovery that
  could rewrite human history, may not be as far from that crisis as it
  appears.

## X. Addendum — Lore Expansions v1.5

The following material expands and deepens several threads established in the
main compendium. It should be read as canonical revision rather than
supplement — where it contradicts earlier implications, the addendum takes
precedence.

### The True Nature of the Predecessors

The Predecessors were not aliens. This is the answer the academic consensus
has never permitted itself to reach, and the answer Bardo is afraid of because
it is the most obvious one once the evidence is read without the assumption
that shaped every question.

The Predecessors were the ancestors of Earth's indigenous peoples — who
achieved interstellar civilisation tens of thousands of years ago, spread
across dozens of systems, built in materials that do not degrade, and left
signals still cycling after fifty millennia. And then, for reasons the ruins
suggest but do not explain, they were gone. Not extinct. Gone.

Much later, the colonial empires rose on Earth. They conquered and suppressed
the peoples who carried the memory of what had come before — in languages, in
oral traditions, in structural patterns that persisted across generations of
erasure. Eventually those empires reached the stars themselves, found
Predecessor sites everywhere, and assumed they were looking at something
alien. Because the alternative was unthinkable. Because the alternative meant
the people they had spent centuries destroying had been to the stars before
them.

Bardo's linguistic correspondences are not mystery. They are inheritance. The
structural patterns in pre-colonial Earth language families and Predecessor
script are the same because they are the same language family, separated by
tens of thousands of years of divergence and deliberate cultural destruction.
The script is not alien. It is ancestral. The signal's message — *we left this
so you would know where to look* — was not addressed to humanity in general.
It was addressed to the people who would still be speaking languages that
carried the echo of it. The Predecessors knew the erasure was coming. They
left a way back.

The ancient aliens trope assumes that any sufficiently impressive ancient
achievement must have come from outside, because we cannot imagine it coming
from the people who were actually there. The Compact's institutional consensus
does the same thing the empires always did: looks at what indigenous peoples
built and decides it must have come from somewhere else. The Predecessor
archive that Alexander has read — the full version, with findings not yet
released — contains enough to make this conclusion unavoidable. That he has
read it and drawn different lessons says something precise about what he
believes the discovery is for.

### The Second Battle of Abraham

The rebellion that broke imperial power on Earth did not begin everywhere at
once. It began in Quebec.

Quebec had been having a version of this argument for centuries — about
sovereignty, language, cultural survival, the right to exist on its own terms
within a structure that was never built for it. The secession that triggered
the collapse of Earth's imperial order was not a revolution. It was a refusal.
A people saying *we are leaving this structure*, and the structure, in trying
to prevent them, revealing what it actually was. The response to Quebec's
secession is what destroyed imperial legitimacy. Quebec just stopped
pretending.

The decisive moment of that secession — the engagement that made it
irreversible — is called the Second Battle of Abraham. The name was chosen
deliberately. The Plains of Abraham, 1759: the battle that determined whether
Quebec would be French or British, that shaped everything that followed about
Quebec's place in the colonial order, the survival of the language, the
centuries of pressure, the sovereignty movement. Quebec lost that battle. The
consequences lasted three hundred years. The name of the second one says: we
know what this is the continuation of. We know what we are finishing.

Other systems joined. Earth held longest. Earth fell last. And when the
Compact moved to consolidate its victory over the Rebellion, they did not
destroy Earth. They understood that obliteration is rage and that rage is not
policy. Instead they destroyed one thing — legible, symbolic, surgical. Quebec
City. A single nuclear strike on a small city, chosen precisely because it was
where it started. The message was not written in communiqués or in the
official record. It was written in the crater.

We can create worlds. We can destroy worlds. The Compact had already
demonstrated the first on Aethon — a capital engineered from an uninhabitable
planet, rivers placed where rivers were placed, weather scheduled in advance.
Quebec City was the second demonstration. Creation and destruction as paired
expressions of the same power. Together they form a complete sentence about
what the Compact believes it is.

The message was received. It didn't work. Not completely. Because Tib exists —
Québécois by heritage and temperament, flying a ship named after a creature
from a folklore they tried to burn, four jumps deep in space the Compact
doesn't control. They made the argument with a nuclear weapon and he answered
it by still being here.

### Tib — What the Name Means

Tib named the ship after the registry was scrubbed. He has not explained why
to anyone's complete satisfaction, including his own. The crew calls her Lou.
He uses the full name when it matters.

The loup-garou is a creature from Québécois folklore specifically — not
generic werewolf mythology, but that tradition, from that place. A creature
that is one thing in the light and another in the dark, that moves between
states without belonging fully to either. The name was Tib's choice after a
city was destroyed and a registry needed replacing and something had to be
called something. He named the ship after a creature from a culture the
Compact burned. He hasn't explained it. He doesn't need to. The name is the
explanation.

The loup-garou is what your culture becomes when the empire tries to destroy
it. Something that lives between states. Something that won't stay dead.
Something that comes back different and doesn't apologise for it.

Tib carries the Second Battle of Abraham the way Quebec carried the first one
— not loudly, not as a speech, but as the thing underneath everything else.
The stubbornness. The dark humour. The loyalty that doesn't announce itself.
He is, in the most literal sense, what survived. He doesn't talk about Quebec
City. The crew knows, in the way crews know things — not from being told, but
from the shape of what isn't said.

He does not yet know that the Predecessors were his ancestors' ancestors. He
does not yet know that the ruins the Compact calls alien are the work of the
people whose secession broke the old world and whose city was erased in
retribution. He is flying a ship named after Québécois folklore four jumps
into uncharted space, and the signal cycling at the Veil site may be pointing
home — to Earth, to the people who carried the memory forward through the
erasure, through the empire, through the crater where Quebec City used to be.

When he finds out, it will matter what he does next.

### The Memorial at Sorrow Station

Sorrow Station has a memorial wall listing 847 names. The compendium notes
that Tove visits it once a transit cycle and does not explain why. This is
part of why.

There is also, in a section of corridor wall not far from the main memorial, a
second, unofficial one. Nobody authorised it. It appeared one day and nobody
removed it because Doss, the station administrator, has a pragmatic
relationship with legal ambiguity and some things are not worth the
conversation. It commemorates Quebec City. It is not large. It does not need
to be.

Tib has never stopped in front of it. Not once, in all the transits through
Sorrow Station, has anyone seen him look at it directly. Tove has. She stops
there too, on the same visit she makes to the main wall. She does not explain
this either, and the crew does not ask.

### What the Rebellion Meant

If the Predecessors are the ancestors of Earth's indigenous peoples, then
Earth's Rebellion against the Compact was not only a political uprising. It
was — possibly without anyone fully understanding it — the people most
connected to the Predecessors asserting themselves at exactly the moment
Predecessor ruins were being found, studied, and controlled by the Compact and
its corporate partners.

Earth's indigenous nations had spent generations rebuilding themselves —
languages restored, erased nations reconstructed, land returned. Without fully
knowing it, they were recovering the thread of something vast that had been
deliberately cut. The Rebellion was the moment that recovery became a
political fact the Compact could not absorb. The Compact suppressed it.
Blockaded the planet. Destroyed the city where it started. And then moved on
to controlling the ruins of the civilisation whose descendants they had just
crushed.

The Compact is not civilisation's steward. It is the latest iteration of the
erasure. Alexander has read the full archive. He knows what the ruins are and
whose they were. He has drawn the conclusion that humanity needs unified
response capacity and that he should be the one to provide it. This is the
same move, made again, by someone who believes he is doing something new.

The signal at the Veil has been cycling for fifty thousand years. It has not
degraded. It has not changed. It is still pointing somewhere. The question the
lore is building toward is not what the Predecessors were. It is whether the
people the Compact spent centuries trying to erase will be the ones who get to
decide what happens when the signal is finally answered.
